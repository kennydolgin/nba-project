Interpretation rule

These visuals describe associations, not causal player impact.

The app compares player-season statistical profiles with team win percentage. It can show which statistical shapes appear more often around stronger teams, but it does not prove that a player or role caused a team to win.

Data scope

What counts as a row?

A player-season or player-team-season row represents a player on one team in one NBA season. If a player changed teams, each team context can appear separately because the team's win percentage and role context changed.

The comparison file used by the app is static/data/player_seasons.csv. It is generated from outputs/tables/top3_h1_player_scatter_data.csv and includes only the fields needed for the interactive site.

Team context

Win percentage and success buckets

win_pct is the team's season win percentage for that player-team-season row. Team win %, team win context, and team context all refer to this same descriptive team-level measure.

Low-winning teams, middle-winning teams, and high-winning teams are win-percentage tertiles. The pipeline ranks team-season win percentages and splits them into three ordered groups with pd.qcut.

Core calculations

Player role metrics

Metric Meaning in the app Why it is used
points_per_36 Scoring, shown as points per 36 minutes. Compares scoring rate without making starters and bench players purely a minutes contest.
points_per_game Traditional points per game. Defines top scorer and gives the familiar baseline that the project moves beyond.
fga_per_game Shot volume, shown as field-goal attempts per game or FGA/g. Separates high-usage scoring roles from lower-shot roles.
efg_pct Shooting efficiency or efficiency, shown as effective field-goal percentage. Accounts for the extra value of made three-point shots when comparing shooting efficiency.
assists_per_36 Creation, shown as assists per 36 minutes. Captures offensive role value that points alone misses.
rebounds_per_36 Rebounding or boards, shown as rebounds per 36 minutes. Represents possession-finishing and frontcourt role context.
stocks_per_36 Defensive events (steals + blocks) per 36 minutes. Summarizes useful box-score defensive event production, but does not capture all defense.
turnovers_per_36 Turnovers per 36 minutes. Shown descriptively. Higher values can reflect more on-ball responsibility as well as mistakes.
role_category_count Role dimensions, role breadth, or breadth. Counts how many role metrics are at or above the sample median: scoring, assists, rebounds, steals, blocks, and eFG%.
minutes_per_game Minutes played per game. Used as a filter in similarity matching and examples so tiny-minute seasons do not dominate.
position_group Broad player position group. Similarity first searches within the same position group when enough comparable rows are available.

Role labels

Profile groups and examples

Role labels are the small tags on each player card. They come from three fields: profile_group, efficient_lower_shot_label, and defensive_event_profile.

profile_group uses role_category_count: 0-1 dimensions is Narrow profile, 2-3 is Mixed profile, and 4-6 is Balanced profile.

efficient_lower_shot_label is Efficient lower-shot player when a player-season is at or above median eFG% and below median FGA/game. On the player cards, rows outside that condition appear as Standard shot profile.

defensive_event_profile compares top-quartile scoring with top-quartile defensive events (steals + blocks). Labels include Top defensive events + top scorer, Top defensive events, not top scorer, Top scorer, not top defensive events, and, on the player cards, Standard defensive-event profile for rows outside those top-quartile categories.

Percentiles

How the fingerprints work

A league percentile compares one selected season with all reviewed player-seasons in the dataset. A 75th percentile scoring value means that season ranked above roughly three quarters of reviewed player-seasons for that metric.

The app uses percentiles in the role fingerprint, archetype radar, role map, and similarity distance. Percentiles are descriptive ranks, not grades.

Interactive comparisons

How each role explorer visual is computed

Role map

The role map was previously called the Winning Profile Galaxy. Each point is one player-season. The x-axis, scoring load percentile, averages points-per-36 percentile and FGA-per-game percentile. The y-axis, non-scoring contribution percentile, averages assists, rebounds, defensive events, eFG%, and role-dimensions percentiles. Color shows team-success bucket. Rings mark Player A and Player B.

Archetype radar

The radar shows selected seasons on six percentile spokes: scoring, creation, boards, events, efficiency, and breadth. The value strip below the chart shows the exact underlying box-score values.

Similar seasons constellation

The constellation shows the closest historical player-season neighbors for each selected season. Nearby labeled names had similar percentile profiles. Similarity distance is Euclidean distance across scoring, shot volume, assists, rebounds, defensive events, eFG%, and role dimensions, after converting each metric to a 0-100 percentile. Smaller distance means more statistically similar. It does not measure player quality or causal impact.

High-winning benchmark bands

For each metric, the shaded band is the middle half of high-winning-team player-seasons, from the 25th percentile to the 75th percentile. The vertical line is the high-winning median. Selected player dots show whether a season sits below, inside, or above that descriptive strong-team range.

Role fingerprint

The fingerprint chart compares Player A and Player B across scoring, creation, rebounding, defensive events, shooting efficiency, shot volume, turnovers, and role dimensions. Bars show league percentile; hover text shows the actual metric value.

Similar-season context and closest matches

The context panel summarizes the nearest 40 comparable player-seasons with median team win percentage and high-winning share. The closest matches table lists the five nearest rows for each selected player, with match name, team, season, and win percentage.

Summary charts

Summary questions, bucket shares, and scatter plots

On the Team Map page, the summary question dropdown changes the bar chart, examples, and scatter plot below the map. The three choices are profile mix by team success, efficient lower-shot contributors, and defensive-event leaders.

The stacked bars calculate shares within each team-success bucket. This prevents high-volume buckets from dominating the display and makes profile mix easier to compare across low-, middle-, and high-winning teams.

The scatter plots show direct bivariate evidence: scoring volume versus team win percentage, shot volume versus effective field-goal percentage, and scoring versus defensive events (steals + blocks). Hovering a point shows the player, season, team, selected x/y values, and label.

Concrete examples are representative rows chosen from the active summary question. They are filtered to players with at least 15 minutes per game and selected to avoid repeating the same team or player when possible.

Map calculation

Team map and state-level win percentage

The NBA Team Context Map uses the season slider to filter team-season rows. Team abbreviations are joined to US states. For states with multiple NBA teams in a season, the displayed value is the average win percentage across those teams.

White states have no NBA team in the selected season. The map tooltip shows state, teams, and average win pct. The side table lists the eight highest state averages for the selected season.

The map gives geographic context for team win percentage, while the main analytical story remains the player-role comparison.

Limitations

What the analysis should not claim

The project should use phrases like "associated with winning," "similar historical player-seasons," and "winning-profile context." It should avoid claims that a role or individual player caused winning unless a separate causal design supports that claim.

Important omitted context includes lineup combinations, opponent strength, injuries, coaching strategy, playoff performance, role changes within a season, and non-box-score defense. The visuals are best read as descriptive evidence for player-role context, not final player rankings.
